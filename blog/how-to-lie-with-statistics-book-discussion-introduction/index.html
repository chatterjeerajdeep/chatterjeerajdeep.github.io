How to Lie with Statistics by Darell Huff - Introduction

A review and discussion of the chapters of the books

Published on Jun 09, 2022

Reading time: 2 minutes.

How to Lie with Statistics is a book by Darell Huff. First published in 1954, this book presents a very easy-going approach to rationally interpret and question the numbers that are advertised everywhere. With plenty of real-life examples, the author highlights how the marketing campaigns use some statistical statements to influence the mass, without any solid grounds to justify the same.

I find it often hard to relate technical terms used in regular text books, to real-life scenerios. This book however takes the opposite path - it starts with examples that are totally relatable and then garnishes them with technicalities. You can simply pick it up and read 10 pages at a stretch without feeling overwhelmed.

Everything makes more sense, when we take the time to think about it. In a series of blogs, I present my musings as I read through each chapter of this book.

Introduction

Percentages are tricky. Probably because while looking at a percentage, we generally don’t focus much on the underlying sample size. I realised this while helping my sister with her exam question that says:

If A is 20% bigger than B, but what % is B smaller than A? and she answered - 20%

Well, my sister made no delay to consider this as the simplest question of the exam and she was confused when her answer turned out to be wrong.

Most of us miss the fact that percentages are relative measurements. If my age is 30 and my sister’s age is 20, the age difference is 10. But mathematically she is 33.34% ( (10/30) * 100) younger than me and I am 50% ( (10/20) * 100) older than her!

The Introduction of this book rightly suggests that averages, relationships, trends and graphs “are not always what they seem”. Often, the use of such statistical data and terminologies in newspaper polls and marketing advertisements appeal to the mass who believe that they are learning facts. But if we do not properly understand the data or if the data does not contain the required details, Huff says, “the result can only be semantic nonsense”.

By calling this book as “a sort of primer in ways to use statistics to deceive”, Huff in light humour indicates how useful this book can be to defend ourselves from “the crooks who already know this tricks”.
