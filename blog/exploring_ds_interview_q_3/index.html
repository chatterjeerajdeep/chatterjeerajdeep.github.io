Exploring Data Science Interview Questions - Day 3

7 interesting questions from Microsoft data science interviews

Published on Jul 12, 2022

Reading time: 3 minutes.

Question 1 - What are Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues?

A matrix represents a linear transformation. When a vector is multiplied to a matrix, it is similar to applying a transformation to the vector, and the result is the transformed vector. If, after the transformation, the vector stays in its span, then it is called an eigenvector.

But, due to the transformation, the vector may be stretched or squished. The amount by which it gets stretched or squished is called the eigenvalue.

A vector that undergoes pure scaling without any rotation, is called the eigen vector. The scaling factor is called the eigen value.

Question 2 - What is Supervised Learning?

When we have labelled examples as our training data, it is called Supervised machine learning. The objective of the machine learning algorithm is to generate predictions as close to the actual labels as possible. The labels not only refer to classes/categories (as in classification problem) but also continuous numbers (as in a regression problem)

Question 3 - What is Unsupervised learning?

When we do not have labelled examples, we just have observation data and we want an algorithm to identify patterns by itself, we call it unsupervised learning. In this type of learning, we generate clusters.

Question 4 - What does “Naive” mean in a Naive Bayes?

The Naive Bayes assumes that the features are independent. This simplifies the math, and is found to provide satifactory results. it is called naive as features may not always be independent, in real world data sets.

The best example, is to explain a spam/ham classification. This algorithm treats each word as independent and hence does not take the sequence of the words into account. But the sequence actually changes the meaning of a sentence. Hence, this algorithm is naive.

Question 5 - Explain a case where a false positive is important than a false negative?

In a spam/ham classification, where spam mail represents the positive class and the ham mail represents the negative class, it is more important to ensure that a ham mail does not end up being marked as a spam. This is because often spam mails are not viewed in the inbox, or sometimes automatically flagged and deleted. This false positive can turn out to be more costly than a spam mail getting classified as a ham mail (false negative).

Question 6 - Explain a case where a false negative important than a false positive?

In classifying a tumour as malignant or benign, malignant being the positive class and bening being the negative class, we do not want a tumour to be falsely classified as benign if it is malignant. This is because, once classified as benign, there won’t be any further tests and the person may die. But if a benign tumour is classified as malignant, then there will be further tests and this classification will be discarded later. There will be additional costs incurred due to further (and unnecessary) testing, but there is no risk of life. Here, a false negative is more important than a false positive.

Question 7 - Explain a case both false positive and false negative are equally important?

For an online streaming platform, determining which customer will churn, is important. This is because the platform then provides additional benefits to the customer to retain them. But additional benefits are costs that the platform has to incur. Considering that a person who churns is positive and who does not churn is negative, both false positives are false negatives are equally important here.

A false positive means, that the platform ends up paying additional benefits to a loyal customer. It is a waste of resources. A false negative means, that the platform loses an existing customer and fails to provide any additional benefits to retain them within time, which is something not desirable.
